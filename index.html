A Teaching Resource · Philosophy of Science

Constructing the Inexplicable

Models, metaphors, and the limits of scientific explanation. A walkthrough from the language we use in science to the images we trust, and why both are constructions.

Central thesis: Scientific explanation is not the revelation of hidden mechanisms. It is the construction of effective linguistic and mathematical tools that compress empirical patterns within specific domains. To teach science well, we must scrutinise that construction.

Lee McCulloch-James · Dwight School London

Part I

What Is a Scientific Explanation?

The language we use in science does more than convey information. It shapes how we understand reality itself.

In science education, students routinely absorb metaphors as literal truths and models as physical realities. When we say heat flows, a student pictures a substance. When we say force causes acceleration, they imagine an agent pushing. When we say capacitors store electricity, they imagine a container full of invisible stuff.

These are not just imprecise phrasings. They are constructions. Every scientific explanation is a compressed, domain-specific, linguistically shaped tool. It works brilliantly within its model. But when exported into everyday speech or transferred across domains without care, it can mislead as easily as it can clarify.

Two thinkers help us see this clearly: Ludwig Wittgenstein and Nancy Cartwright. Not because they agree with each other, but because each, in their own way, challenges the idea that scientific explanation is ever neutral, universal, or free of linguistic baggage.

Part II

A Taxonomy of Explanation

Science is often presented as if every explanation were mechanistic. It isn't.

One reason students get confused is that science education tends to present one mode of explanation as if it were the only mode. But scientific explanations come in several distinct types, and recognising which type you're using is the first step to understanding its limits.

TypeWhat it doesExample
MechanisticDescribes a causal process step by stepEnzyme catalysis; synaptic transmission
Model-basedExplains within an idealised framework + assumptionsSnell's law; F = ma in inertial frames
StatisticalAccounts for large-scale regularities from ensemblesEntropy increase; Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution
Symmetry-basedDerives constraints from invariance principlesNoether's theorem; conservation laws
PhenomenologicalFits behaviour without deep ontologyDark matter models; rotation curve fits
ComputationalReproduces behaviour via simulationClimate models; neural network classification
Why does this matter? When a student asks "but what really happens?", they usually want a mechanistic answer. But the explanation they've been given may be statistical, or symmetry-based, or phenomenological. Knowing the type helps you understand what it can and cannot deliver.
Part III

Two Philosophical Lenses

One asks what the words are doing. The other asks where the model breaks down.

Wittgenstein · Language-Games

In Philosophical Investigations (1953), Wittgenstein argues that meaning lives in use, not in definition. Just as the word king means something different in chess, politics, and fairy tales, terms like force or energy only make sense within the specific "game" of doing physics. His question: what work is the word doing here?

Cartwright · The Laws Lie

In How the Laws of Physics Lie (1983), Cartwright argues that laws like F = ma are not universal truths but effective rules embedded in specific models. They gain power from how well they can be bridged to actual systems, not from their universality. Her question: in what domain does this explanation actually hold?

Between them, they give us a two-pronged diagnostic. For any scientific statement, ask:

Wittgenstein: Is the language importing everyday meaning into a technical context? Is metaphor doing the work that mechanism should?

Cartwright: Under what specific conditions, in what model, with what idealisations does this statement hold? What happens at the boundaries?

Part IV

Seeing as Construction

Scientific images are not windows onto reality. They are assembled outputs of instruments, processing, and interpretive conventions.

In the striking footage from MIT's Camera Culture group, a light pulse appears to pass through a Coke bottle in slow motion. The wavefront seems to bend and ripple through the glass in real time.

But what we are watching is not raw reality. It is a reconstructed composite, assembled from millions of repetitions of a brief event, each captured incrementally through scattered photon arrivals and compiled into a coherent visualisation.

Femto-photography: a light pulse moving through a Coke bottle at an effective trillion frames per second. Each frame is compiled from millions of laser firings. Watch the full TED talk. Credit: MIT Media Lab, Camera Culture Group / media.mit.edu

The Wedding Photographer Analogy

Imagine a wedding photographer trying to take a perfect group photo of 100 guests. In every shot, someone blinks, moves, or looks away. So the photographer takes 50 different photos, hoping that for each guest at least one catches them at their best. The final "photo" is a collage: each person shown at their sharpest moment, though no single exposure caught everyone perfectly.

Femto-photography works the same way. A laser is fired through the bottle millions of times. A streak camera captures how scattered photons arrive at different spatial and temporal positions. Each run captures a fragment. When compiled, these fragments reconstruct what looks like a real-time slow-motion video of light in flight.

Key insight: In both cases we do not record an event in real time. We build it, frame by frame, from statistically reliable pieces, revealing something we could never see directly.

From Cameras to Retinas

The human eye does not watch photons travel in real time either. When a photon hits the retina, it is absorbed by a photoreceptor, triggering a quantum event that launches a chain of biochemical and electrical signals. The brain assembles these into a stable visual experience. Seeing is not passive reception. It is active interpretation.

Cartwright

These images, both technological and biological, are effective within their domains, but they do not necessarily reveal the true ontology of light or matter. They are tools of description, not metaphysical certainties.

Wittgenstein

We say we "see" a wavefront in a reconstruction, and we "see" a tree in the park. But the meaning of "see" is shaped by context, not by essence. The word operates within a language-game; its significance derives from how we use it.

Part V

A Beam, Not The Beam

The Pale Blue Dot and the rainbow: when what we "see" is contingent on where we stand.

Pale Blue Dot: Earth as less than a pixel, apparently suspended in a diagonal shaft of light. Voyager 1, 14 February 1990, approximately 6 billion km from Earth. Credit: NASA/JPL-Caltech. photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov

This image has become iconic. But what exactly are we seeing? The diagonal streak, the so-called "sunbeam", is not a literal beam of sunlight travelling across space. It is an optical artefact. When Voyager's camera turned near the direction of the Sun, sunlight scattered inside the camera's optics, producing lens flare: a streak of light caused by internal reflection, not by an object in space.

We are seeing light reflected off Earth travelling into the camera, superimposed on a streak created by the camera itself. The beam is not "out there" in a fixed place. It is a beam, not the beam: a localised phenomenon contingent on observer, angle, and optics.

The Rainbow Analogy

A rainbow is not a unique physical arc hanging in the sky. It is an observer-dependent phenomenon arising from light refracting and reflecting through raindrops in relation to an observer's position. If someone says "look at the rainbow", unweave it by reminding them it is their rainbow, or a rainbow. You don't see the same rainbow someone else sees. Each rainbow is personal, geometrically unique.

In the same way, the "sunbeam" in the Pale Blue Dot image is an optical construction, a byproduct of viewing conditions. What we see is always assembled. It is a synthesis of incoming light, interpreted through apparatus, whether that's a camera or a retina.

The poetic phrase "Earth caught in a sunbeam" evokes a constructed truth: that our images, and our perceptions, are never in the raw. They are shaped by instruments, perspectives, and the models we use to interpret them.

Part VI

Explanatory Compression

The hinge concept that unifies the whole piece.

Scientific explanations often work because they compress enormous complexity into portable, usable forms. The price of portability is abstraction, idealisation, and metaphor. "Heat flows from hot to cold" is not a revelation of microscopic reality. It is a compact linguistic compression of vast ensembles of molecular behaviour under a constrained thermodynamic description.

Compressed statementWhat it hidesWhy it survives
Heat flows from hot to coldMicroscopic collisions, statistical mechanics, boundary assumptionsQuick predictive usefulness
Opposite charges attractField formalism, distance dependence, medium effectsReliable first-pass rule
Dark matter holds galaxies togetherInference from rotational data, competing models, indirect ontologyNarrative clarity and predictive fit
Entropy is disorderMicrostate counting, isolation conditions, information-theoretic definitionsVivid, memorable image
The key question for students: Ask not just "is this true?" but "what complexity has been compressed into this sentence, and what assumptions are hidden by the compression?"
Part VII

The Three-Step Method

A practical tool for deconstructing scientific language in the classroom.

Rather than merely criticising textbook language, we can offer a method that teachers and students can use immediately on any scientific statement.

1

Textbook Phrasing

State the ordinary simplified explanation exactly as it is usually taught.

2

Mechanistic Reconstruction

Rewrite it in more precise physical, chemical, or biological terms. Name the model and its conditions.

3

Philosophical Critique

Ask what Cartwright and Wittgenstein would say about its domain, its language, and its implied ontology.

Worked Examples

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

Newton's first law: in the absence of a net external force, velocity remains constant. No teleology, no desire. No force means no acceleration.

Step 3a: Cartwright

This anthropomorphises inertia. Objects have no "desires." The law is a model idealisation describing motion when forces cancel.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Wants" borrows a human metaphor. It blurs the line between the physicist's use of language and ordinary speech.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

Net thermal energy transfer proceeds from a higher-temperature body to a lower-temperature body until equilibrium. A consequence of the second law and the statistical behaviour of many particles.

Step 3a: Cartwright

Assumes ideal conditions, closed system, no external work. With a heat pump, heat can flow cold to hot. The "spontaneous" phrasing smuggles in a mysterious force of hotness that does not exist.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Flows" is a metaphor from fluids. "Spontaneously" implies intent. These terms conceal that heat is energy transfer, not a substance.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

A capacitor stores electric potential energy in the electric field between charged plates. It holds equal and opposite charges. It does not store a substance called "electricity."

Step 3a: Cartwright

What is really stored is energy, modelled by Q = CV. "Electricity" lacks a precise definition, making this misleading within any careful model.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Stores electricity" borrows everyday container language. It invites a substance picture that the physics does not support.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

Observed galactic rotation curves and gravitational lensing are commonly modelled by positing additional non-luminous mass beyond visible baryonic matter. The inference is indirect.

Step 3a: Cartwright

Assumes Newtonian gravity on galactic scales. The inference is model-based and not directly observed. The "holding together" is an explanatory construction.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Holds together" anthropomorphises galaxies. "Dark matter" is a placeholder in a model, a nominative for a verb we haven't yet learned to conjugate.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

Interference patterns arise when coherent light sources overlap. Wave amplitudes superpose, yielding constructive and destructive regions, as in the double-slit experiment.

Step 3a: Cartwright

Assumes coherent source and phase stability. In decoherent conditions interference fails. The explanation is valid only within controlled optical domains.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Interferes with itself" is paradoxical if taken literally. It leads to confusion if one pictures photons splitting and meeting themselves.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

Only photons above a threshold frequency eject electrons. Increasing intensity below threshold has no effect. Explained by Einstein's photon hypothesis (1905).

Step 3a: Cartwright

This model works well here but not in optical fibre design or diffraction. Its success is domain-specific, not a universal proof of quantisation.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Proves" imports a certainty the evidence does not universally warrant. This is an inference within a model, not a metaphysical leap.

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

A metaphor mapping current → flow rate, voltage → pressure, resistance → pipe width. Gives intuitive access to Ohm's law but breaks down with fields, discrete charges, and high-frequency behaviour.

Step 3a: Cartwright

Useful but limited. Ignores signal propagation speed, field-mediated energy transfer, and quantum effects. Overreliance obscures actual electrical behaviour.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

Electrons aren't fluid. Examining the analogy's limits is exactly where clarity begins. What does "flows" do in this sentence?

Step 1 → Step 2: Mechanistic

E = mc² relates mass and energy within special relativity. Mass can be converted to energy in nuclear reactions. They form a conserved quantity within the theory.

Step 3a: Cartwright

Holds within relativity. Outside that model, "mass" and "energy" can diverge in meaning. Domain-specificity matters.

Step 3b: Wittgenstein

"Equivalent" can confuse unless defined. It is a relation between quantities in a formula, not an everyday equivalence.

Part VIII

Student Exercises

Turn the philosophy into practice.

Exercise A: Rewrite the Phrase

Choose one of the following statements and apply Steps 1 and 2 of the method. Rewrite it in more precise terms, naming the model and its conditions.

"Light bends when it enters glass."  •  "The battery pushes electrons around the circuit."  •  "Genes tell cells what to do."  •  "Cold is the absence of heat."

Exercise B: Identify the Compression

For each statement, say (a) what complexity is being compressed and (b) what assumptions are hidden.

"Entropy is disorder."  •  "Opposite charges attract."  •  "Evolution selects the fittest."  •  "Moving clocks run slower."

Exercise C: Cartwright or Wittgenstein?

For each critique below, decide: is the main issue one of model-domain (Cartwright), language use (Wittgenstein), or both?

"Current flows like water."  •  "Energy is always conserved."  •  "The brain sees the world directly."  •  "An object resists changes in motion."

Exercise D: Image Critique

Choose one scientific image: an fMRI brain scan, an electron micrograph, the Hubble Deep Field, or the Pale Blue Dot. Explain what the viewer thinks they see and what is actually constructed.

Coda

Nominatives for Verbs

It was in order to provide a nominative for the verb 'to undulate'.

Lord Balfour, on the invention of the aether

Balfour's quip about the aether applies equally to every candidate for the "bottom" in modern physics. The string is a nominative for the verb "to satisfy the equations of motion." The loop is a nominative for the verb "to close under holonomy." The twistor is a nominative for the verb "to map null geodesics to projective space."

Cartwright would say: these are effective tools, not real entities. Their power comes from domain-specific bridging. Wittgenstein would say: the confusion is grammatical. We look for a thing the word names. But the meaning lives in the calculations we perform with it.

Science succeeds not because it strips away every veil and presents reality untouched, but because it builds disciplined forms of speech, mathematics, and imagery that compress the world well enough to predict, intervene, and teach.

The most material of scientific ambitions has produced the most immaterial of ontologies. At the bottom of the reductionist rabbit hole, there is no bottom. There is only mathematics, winking back.
